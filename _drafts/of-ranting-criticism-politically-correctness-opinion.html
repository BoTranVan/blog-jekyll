They say in the course of study one must pass these three stages:

  1. Beginner: realizes how much she has yet to learn.
  2. Experienced: realizes she has come a long way; that she's now in good control of the subject. She has firm opinions.
  3. Very experienced: realizes just how much, much more she has yet to learn.

When conducting MySQL consulting or training, I'm often asked as for my opinions. At times I provide them without being asked. Some opinions just cannot be delivered while being politically correct. Most often, I believe, they shouldn't be politically correct.

A common example

Let's discuss the very common question: MyISAM or InnoDB?

I have an opinion. It is based on my experience. I am also aware that my experience may differ for that of other people. When I discuss MyISAM vs. InnoDB, I usually begin with MyISAM. It kind of draws fire: no crash recovery; no transactions; table level locks; expensive key flush; no integrated hot backup; no foreign keys... I have some horror stories to tell.

OMG, people say, faces whitening. Why would anyone use MyISAM in the first place?

Well, for many good reasons, I tell them. Most notable, it works for them. Unconvinced, we move on to discussing InnoDB. Aha! Crash recovery; hot backups; fully ACID compliant; MVCC!

Oh, no integrated fulltext search (MyISAM or 3rd party solutions required). Locks & deadlocks! More difficult to understand what's going on. So many configuration parameters - what should we choose? More disk space! Plugin has compression but is yet not GA; and it takes so much longer to write compressed data...

Faces whitening again...

Is this a fair comparison?

You can't just make up feature lists for the two and see who's got more.Â Once must decide what his objectives are.

Do you have to have transactions? Do you have to have good crash recovery? Set the requirements; the rest will follow.

Criticism or blaming or bashing?

It is part of my job to illustrate the problems/downsides. Reading up on the advantages is relatively easy. Open the documentation; or a simple blog post; read a book! The downsides are less easy to come by. Some are well documented and clear. Others come from experience. My experience, customers' experience, students' experience.

So at times I may sound negative, because I'm mentioning a lot of downsides. This can also reflect in my blog posts. Should this be considered as bashing? No!

At times, it may sound like ranting; sometimes it is: one may get frustrated with a problem; one rants. One can criticize. The good kind of criticism, that is: the kind of which you provide with insight, with suggestions.

I reject bashing, in the form of "MySQL ****s, use PostgreSQL!". It's just too... Looking for the right word...

Some confuse this with: "Well, you make a living of MySQL. You're biased!" (I actually got told that once, and so I believe thought at many times). Ouch! How did I get to be in that position? Does the fact I have Ubuntu mean I don't approve of KDE? Using bash, does that make me an enemy of csh people? Fill in the right word again...

Not a MySQL thing

With every single technology I ever worked with; with every programming language; every platform; every library - there was a lot to criticize.

You work with something - you find problems. It's the way of things (also true outside the computer world, I'm told). You also find beauty.

You keep working with that something - then it probably fits your needs. You may even like it!
